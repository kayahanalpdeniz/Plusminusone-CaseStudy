Section 01
Competitor Analysis & Funnel Reverse Engineering
We went through each competitor's funnel end to end — not just reading about them, but actually walking through the flows as a user would. The observations below are firsthand.
Noom
Psychology-based weight loss · Subscription-first · ~$70/month
Target audience: Adults who've tried dieting before and failed — seeking a psychology-backed, "this time will be different" solution. Primarily 30–55, skews female.
Traffic est. via SimilarWeb · Revenue figures from Axios/Bloomberg reports (2021–22 peak; current figures not publicly disclosed)
~3M monthly visits
~$700M+ reported revenue
(2021–22 peak period)
Ad→ Quiz Landing→ Long Multi-Branch Quiz→ Loading→ Plan Preview→ Web Paywall→ App Handoff ⚠

Walk through Noom's quiz and the first thing you notice is the tone. No matter what you answer — including pretty dark responses about emotional eating or negative body image — the product never makes you feel bad about it. It reframes, adjusts, and keeps moving forward. For an app targeting people with complicated relationships with food, that's the right instinct, and they execute it consistently throughout.

The quiz is long, branching based on your answers, and includes mini-lessons on nutrition and behavioral psychology alongside the standard intake questions. According to FunnelFox's public analysis of Noom's funnel (which tested ~20 different paths), the quiz spans roughly 70–100+ screens depending on your path — some users see more, some fewer. The specific count isn't the point; the point is that by the end, you've spent real time with the product, and that investment creates genuine conversion momentum. The loading screen that appears afterward — "building your plan" — then shifts the psychological contract: you feel like something was done for you, not just collected from you.

But then Noom completely breaks at the handoff. After going through the full web quiz on a desktop browser — however many screens that turns out to be — the final page we landed on said "Please open this email link on your mobile device." No QR code. No link to copy. No email input to send yourself something. Just that message, and a phone that wasn't in our hand. For anyone who found Noom through organic search on desktop, the entire funnel ends there. All of that investment, evaporated.

✓ What Works
Non-judgmental tone throughout — reframes even negative answers constructively, never shames
Quiz includes actual nutrition education — users feel like they're learning, not just being profiled
Specific-date weight prediction lands at exactly the right moment, after all that quiz investment
The "building your plan" loading screen is a smart psychological pivot — shifts from data collection to value delivery
✗ Where It Fails
Web-to-app handoff is completely broken for desktop users — no QR code, no link, no email option
Quiz length creates drop-off risk — the investment effect cuts both ways
"Psychology-based" positioning has become a category default — every competitor uses similar language now
AI appears nowhere in the product positioning — a significant missed opportunity in 2025

BetterMe
Multi-segment funnels · Web paywall model · Avoids App Store fees
Target audience: Women 18–50 interested in weight loss, with separate funnels for pilates, meal planning, and wellness. Male users and non-weight-loss goals are largely absent from their funnel.
Funnel structure based on firsthand testing across multiple ad entry points · 12+ funnel variants confirmed via FunnelFox research
12+ active funnel variants
~$39/month average
Web paywall → App Store bypass
Segment Ad→ Demographic Landing→ Short Quiz→ Avatar + Health Profile→ Prediction Graph→ Web Paywall→ App (plan active)

BetterMe runs a more sophisticated operation than it looks. When we clicked on different ads — a high-protein meal plan ad, a pilates ad, a "weight loss after 40" ad — we arrived at completely different landing pages, different quiz flows, different avatar styles, different copy. Same underlying app, but twelve separate entry experiences tailored to different audiences. That level of funnel discipline is rare and expensive to maintain, but it clearly works at their scale.

Two moments in their flow are genuinely smart. The 3D avatar that gets generated from your body metrics is a bit gimmicky but surprisingly effective — it makes your health profile feel like something that belongs to you, not just a database entry. And the weight prediction graph that follows — "By December 20, we estimate you'll reach 86 kg" — ties a specific date to a specific number, which is much harder to dismiss than a vague "lose weight in weeks" promise. Both of these are worth adapting.

The problem is narrow. Every avatar was female. Every photo in the age selection screen was a woman in athletic wear. We clicked through multiple funnel variants — high protein, general fitness, meal planning — and found no male representation anywhere. There was also no path for users who wanted to gain weight, gain muscle, or simply track macros without a weight-loss goal. That's half the potential user base receiving a clear signal that this product wasn't designed for them.

✓ What Works
12+ separate funnel variants — same app, but each audience segment gets a fully tailored entry experience
3D avatar makes the health profile feel personal and concrete rather than abstract
"By December 20, you'll reach 86 kg" — specific date + specific number is far more compelling than vague promises
Web paywall avoids the App Store 30% fee — financially the smarter model at scale
✗ Where It Fails
Every avatar and photo is female — male users get an immediate "this isn't for me" signal
Goal options are exclusively weight loss — muscle gain, weight gain, and macro tracking don't exist in their funnel
Countdown timers on the paywall feel artificial and increasingly distrust-generating with savvy users
Before/after imagery is losing credibility — younger audiences are skeptical of transformation claims
🔍 First-Hand Observation
We ran through BetterMe's "high-protein meal planning" funnel. Age selection, avatar, and all quiz visuals featured exclusively women. There was no option to indicate a male body type or select goals beyond weight loss. Goals like gaining muscle, gaining weight, or macro tracking were entirely absent — a significant portion of the fitness market with no path in.
⚠ Weak Point: Women-Only Representation
Every age bracket is represented with female photos only. Male users receive an immediate visual signal that this product wasn't designed for them — a large segment of the potential market quietly excluded.
✓ Strong Point: Personalized Health Profile
BMI slider with a real-time position indicator, body type classification, and a generated 3D avatar — all shown together. Makes the profile feel tangible and personal rather than a form you're filling out.
✓ Strong Point: Specific-Date Prediction
"By December 20, we estimate you'll reach 86 kg." A named date plus a specific number — this is one of the most effective conversion moments in any funnel. It makes the goal feel real and achievable.

Target audience: Broadest in the category — anyone tracking food intake across all goals. Strong retention with habitual loggers; struggles to acquire new users who don't already know them.
User count from Under Armour/MFP official reports · App Store ranking data from Sensor Tower · No formal web funnel — organic traffic goes to marketing site then App Store
220M+ users

MyFitnessPal's biggest strength isn't the product — it's the moat. 220 million users, a food database built over fifteen years, chart positions that act as passive advertising, and a brand name that's genuinely embedded in culture. A new app can't match that directly, and it would be a mistake to try.

What MFP does well that's actually worth noting: the goal selection screen. "Lose weight, maintain weight, gain weight, gain muscle, modify my diet, manage stress, increase step count" — seven distinct options, all treated as equally valid. Most competitors act like weight loss is the only legitimate reason to use a nutrition app. MFP doesn't, and that breadth is one of the reasons the product has stayed relevant across such a wide demographic.

Everything else is a problem. There's no web funnel — web traffic routes directly to the marketing site and then the App Store with no quiz, no personalization, no plan preview. You download the app having no idea what you're getting. The UI hasn't meaningfully changed since around 2015, which at this point signals a team that's stopped investing in the experience. And in the free tier, you'll see ads — in a health app, for food products, which is an actively hostile user experience that MFP appears to have made a permanent business decision around.

✓ What Works
Broadest goal coverage in the category — weight gain and muscle gain on equal footing with weight loss
Brand authority and SEO moat built over 15+ years — essentially impossible to replicate quickly
Freemium habit formation: users build logging streaks before the premium upgrade ask ever appears
14M+ food database globally — unmatched coverage, especially for international markets
✗ Where It Fails
No web funnel at all — organic traffic drops cold into App Store with no personalization or plan preview
UI design unchanged since ~2015 — signals low investment in user experience
Ads in the free tier actively work against the app's stated purpose of healthy eating
No AI feature — manual calorie entry is still the core mechanic in 2025

But immediately after that strong opening, the funnel jumps straight to account creation: email, password, terms checkbox. No plan preview, no "here's what you'll get," no personalization reveal. The product asks for your commitment before it's earned any of it.
✓ Strong Point: Broad Goal Coverage
Seven goal options — including "Gain weight" and "Gain muscle" at the same level as "Lose weight." This is rarer than it should be. Most competitors treat muscle gain and weight gain as afterthoughts or ignore them entirely.
⚠ Weak Point: Cold Account Creation, No Plan Preview
After the goal selection, MFP jumps straight to email + password. No personalized plan, no preview of what's coming, no value moment. Users are asked for commitment before the product has done anything for them.

4 Strategic Opportunities
01
AI-first positioning is wide open
None of the three surface AI as their primary value proposition in the web funnel. "Snap your meal, AI does the rest" is an unoccupied lane — and it's exactly what differentiates EatLike from every manual-entry tool in the market.
02
A snap demo inside the quiz
Showing users how the AI actually works during the quiz — before they pay or download — is something no competitor does. One screen with a meal photo analyzed in real time makes the product tangible in a way that copy alone never can.
03
Intelligence over quiz length
Noom converts through sunk cost from a long quiz. A 15–20 screen quiz that branches intelligently can deliver equivalent personalization with far less drop-off. Efficiency isn't a compromise — it's a differentiator in a category known for exhausting onboarding.
04
Clean premium positioning by default
MFP shows ads in a health app. BetterMe uses timers that feel fake. Noom charges $70/month and still feels complicated. A calm, AI-native, freemium product with honest pricing occupies the upper tier of this market almost by default.

Pricing Logic & Conversion Tactics
1
Three-tier anchoring
Three options make the middle feel like the rational choice — not too cheap (feels low quality) and not the most expensive. "Most Popular" badge adds social proof without fabricating urgency.
2
7-day free trial removes risk
The CTA sells the trial, not the subscription. "Start 7-Day Free Trial" is psychologically easier to click than "Subscribe." Users experience value first; the charge comes after they've already committed behaviorally.
3
30-day guarantee handles churn fear
The Returner persona's biggest objection: "I'll pay and quit again." A money-back guarantee directly addresses this. "One tap" cancel signals confidence in the product — not a trap.
4
Testimonial above the paywall
The quote is from The Returner — the persona most likely to hesitate. Someone who tried the same competing apps and stayed this time. Placed before the pricing to reduce resistance before the ask.
5
Upsell trigger context matters
This paywall appears at the moment of maximum frustration — when the user just hit their snap limit or saw the macro lock. That's intentional. The upgrade ask lands when the value of Premium is most tangible.
Section 04
A/B Test Proposals
Two tests targeting the funnel's two most impactful points. Test 1 is structural — it addresses the single biggest drop-off. Test 2 is a quick win that can be shipped in a day and produces results in under a week.
Test 1 — Email Gate Position
High Priority · Run First
"Users aren't willing to give their email before they've seen what they're getting. If we show the personalized plan first and make email capture a natural 'save this to your app' moment rather than a gatekeeper, submission rate will improve — because we've earned the ask."
📊 Basis for estimates: The "+10–15 percentage point" estimate for Variant B comes from FunnelFox benchmark data showing that "value-first email capture" (show result, then ask) outperforms "email gate" (ask before showing result) by 30–50% on average in health and fitness funnels. Applied to a 25% baseline: a 30% improvement = +7–8pp, a 50% improvement = +12–13pp. The range of +10–15pp is the middle of that observed range, not a guarantee — the actual number will be measured by the test.

How to Analyze Results & Iterate

Running a test produces a result. The result tells you something about user behavior — but only if you read it in context. Here's how each possible outcome maps to a next action, and how the two tests feed into a broader iterative sequence.

Test 1 Outcomes — Email Gate Position
✓
Variant B wins + guardrails hold: Ship it. The hypothesis "show value first, ask second" is confirmed. Next test: Plan Preview Content Depth — does showing the weight prediction graph vs. just the calorie target lift email rate further?
⚠
Variant B wins but email bounce rises: Mixed result. More emails, lower quality. The lower-friction gate is attracting casual submitters. Try Variant C next — partial blur adds just enough friction to self-select higher-intent users.
→
No clear winner: Gate position isn't the root cause. Look upstream — if quiz completion is below 55%, the quiz itself is losing users before the plan preview. Fix that first.
✗
Control wins: The current gate is working. The 75% drop-off is a volume problem, not a friction problem. Investigate whether the plan preview itself is compelling enough — the chart or macro values may not be resonating.
Test 2 Outcomes — Hero CTA Copy
✓
Variant B wins ("Start for free"): "Free" framing reduces commitment perception. Update all CTAs consistently across the funnel. Next test: hero subtext — does changing the line below the headline also move the needle?
⚠
Quiz completion drops as guardrail: "Start for free" attracts a less-motivated audience. The CTA is working for volume but not quality. Consider running the test only on paid traffic where intent is already higher.
→
No clear winner: CTA copy isn't the bottleneck at this stage of growth. The landing page hero message itself — not just the button — may be the issue. Broaden the test to include the full headline, not just the CTA.

How This Dashboard Drives Decisions
📅 Weekly Review (Monday morning)
→Check quiz_start_rate and email_submit_rate vs. prior week. Any drop >3pp triggers investigation — look at breakdown by channel first.
→Review creative performance. If any format's email CVR drops below 20%, pause it and reallocate budget to the top performer.
→Check A/B test status. If neither variant has reached 1,800 plan_preview_views, continue. If one has cleared significance, document result and ship winner.
→Monitor payback signal per acquisition cohort. If this quarter's cohort is tracking faster than last quarter, CPI budget can increase.
🚨 When to Act Immediately
!D7 retention drops below 35% — funnel is attracting wrong-fit users. Pause paid spend. Review creative targeting before expanding budget.
!Email bounce rate exceeds 7% during Test 1 — Variant B is collecting low-quality leads. Mark as guardrail failure even if email CVR is higher.
!ROAS on any channel falls below 0.8x — reduce that channel's budget to floor and investigate landing page-to-quiz CVR for that traffic source.
!quiz_completion_rate drops below 50% — a quiz question is causing drop-off. Use session recordings to identify the exit point and remove or rephrase the question.
